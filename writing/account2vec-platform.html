Why we built Account2Vec as a
platform, not a model.

A lot of ML work inside big orgs stays trapped in one team's notebook. The trick to turning a model into a platform primitive is almost never the model — it's the feature matrix, the drift plumbing, and the design doc that names the abstraction.

TL;DR

The autoencoder was the easy part. What made Account2Vec a platform — and not a slide in someone's OKR review — was naming the abstraction, owning the feature matrix, treating drift monitoring as day-zero work, and writing the design doc before the code. If one model becomes the primitive ten downstream teams build on, you've shipped a platform. Otherwise you've shipped a demo.

Every large org has the same graveyard. Notebooks with beautiful t-SNE plots. Models with a training script in team-a/src/, a scoring script in team-b/notebooks/, and no one who remembers which one went to prod. The graveyard is almost never the model's fault. It's the fault of the thing around the model.

When we started Account2Vec, the fleet already had four or five half-finished attempts to learn account representations. All of them were technically fine. None of them had shipped. That was the problem to solve — not "train a better encoder."

The notebook-in-one-team trap.

You know the pattern. A strong IC on team A trains an autoencoder on 100 behavioral signals per account. The results look great. They brief the org. Team B says "can we get the embeddings?" Team A says yes, and then spends the next six weeks in meetings about scoring cadence, backfill, schema, and who owns the pager.

The embeddings never actually land in team B's pipeline. Team C sees this, decides "we'll just build our own," and starts from raw telemetry. The org ends up with three identical models and zero platform.

The failure mode isn't modeling. It's that no one wrote down the interface. There's no versioned artifact, no SLA, no named abstraction anyone can cite in a design doc. There's just an IC and a notebook.

Name the abstraction first.

The single highest-leverage decision on this project was naming the thing.

Account2Vec is a per-storage-account embedding. Subscription2Vec pools it to the subscription level. Customer2Vec (or TPID2Vec internally) pools to the paying customer. Three tiers, three names, one doc that explains when to use which.

If downstream teams can't say the name of your abstraction in a meeting, it doesn't exist yet.

Naming sounds like marketing. It isn't. A name is the slot in every future design doc that says "we'll consume X from the platform." Without the name, every team that wants to use your model has to re-explain it from scratch — and most of them give up and rebuild.

Once Account2Vec showed up in three or four design reviews I didn't write, the platform existed.

The feature matrix is the product.

The thing no one wants to own — and the thing the platform lives or dies on — is the feature matrix.

For us that was the AccountFeatureMatrix: a rolling 28-day rectangular table, one row per account, ~50 columns spanning transaction rates, capacity trajectories, auth patterns, reliability signals, latency percentiles, config churn, and a few metadata columns. Not 100 raw metrics. Fifty engineered features, each with a defended reason to exist.

Why this is the highest-leverage work on the whole project:

  • Every downstream model reads the same columns in the same order from the same source. There is no ambiguity about what "auth failure rate" means in team A's model vs team C's.
  • Feature drift gets monitored once, at the matrix level, not per-consumer. One dashboard flags the issue for every downstream at once.
  • Schema changes are governed. Adding a column is a PR with a reviewer. Removing one requires a deprecation window. That's what turns features into an API.
  • Backfill is centralized. When we added a new signal last quarter, every team got it the next day — no one had to rerun their own pipeline.

This work is deeply unglamorous. Nobody puts "designed the feature pipeline" on their principal-eng nomination. But if you look at a fleet-scale ML team that actually ships, you'll find exactly one person whose real job is owning this matrix, and they're the reason anything works.

Drift plumbing is a day-zero feature.

The wrong time to build drift monitoring is after a downstream team pages you at 2am asking why their anomaly scores are weird.

By that point you've already lost the trust that makes a platform work. A platform isn't "the model is correct today." It's "when the model stops being correct, you'll know before anyone else does."

What we wired from day zero: 

# per-feature, per-embedding-dim, nightly
checks = [
    "coverage",      # % of accounts with non-null values
    "staleness",     # lag between event time & feature-matrix write
    "distribution",  # PSI vs 28-day baseline, per column
    "embedding_drift" # centroid shift in z-space,
]

for check in checks:
    result = run(check)
    if result.breached(threshold):
        alert(check, severity=result.severity)

None of those checks are novel. The point is that they existed on day one, not day three hundred. The cost of adding them early is low; the cost of adding them retroactively — after downstream teams have already eaten a bad training run — is incalculable.

The hierarchy is load-bearing.

Most orgs need embeddings at multiple granularities: account for reliability work, subscription for billing, customer (TPID) for exec reporting. The naive move is to train three separate autoencoders. Don't.

We trained one encoder on accounts and pooled up. Not a naive average — an attention-weighted pool, so that a noisy subscription with one very anomalous account doesn't drown the signal: 

def pool_to_subscription(account_embeds, weights):
    # weights = softmax over account importance
    # (capacity-weighted, not row-count-weighted)
    return (account_embeds * weights).sum(dim=0)

Two reasons this is the right call:

  • Coherence. Three separate encoders diverge over time — a similarity claim at the customer level stops implying anything at the account level. With pooling, the hierarchy is algebraically consistent.
  • Cost. One model to retrain, one to monitor, one to debug. The pooling layers are cheap.

The design doc is the real artifact.

The code was maybe 20% of the project. The model was maybe 10%. The design doc was where most of the leverage came from.

Not the pretty one-pager — the long one. The one that covers the motivation in plain English, the feature-matrix schema, the training regime, the hierarchy pooling math, the monitoring plan, and the two or three things we explicitly decided not to do.

That last part matters more than people admit. Every platform has a trail of "why didn't we do X?" questions that'll get asked in every review for the next two years. Writing down the answer once, with the context you had at the time, saves you from re-litigating the decision from scratch every quarter.

The doc is also the thing that makes the platform legible to people who weren't in the room. A new team that wants to build on Account2Vec shouldn't need to schedule a 1:1. They should read the doc, skim the schema, and start.

A platform is a model plus an interface plus an SLA plus a doc. If you only ship the model, you have a prototype. If you ship all four, you've built something other teams can stand on — and that's the only way fleet-scale ML actually compounds inside a big org.

The unglamorous work is the work. Own the feature matrix. Name the abstraction. Write the doc.
